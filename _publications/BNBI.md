Peer review is the most important method of scientific publication. For a long time, scientific status has been regarded as a manifestation of “legitimacy,” and editors and reviewers’ decisions on papers may be influenced by it, yet conclusive evidence has been lacking. This study presents the first large-scale empirical investigation into how scientific status influences the duration of the publishing process, using prizewinning as a proxy for status. Integrating data from PubMed and OpenAlex, we constructed a dataset of 1,168,808 publication records of 1778 prizewinners and their 102,007 collaborators. Employing fixed-effects (FE) regression and difference-in-differences (DID) event-study designs, we isolated causal effects while controlling for field, journal, author experience, time trends, etc. Results reveal a transient “halo effect”: papers by prizewinners experience significantly faster publishing times than those by their coauthors. This advantage, however, decays monotonically over time and is more prominent in prestigious journals (e.g., Nature Index journals) compared to lower-tier outlets. FE regression analyses confirm that journal impact factor, team size, and reference count positively correlate with longer review times, whereas higher citation counts predict faster processing. Additionally, younger winners in elite journals benefit more from accelerated reviews. DID event studies further validate these dynamic patterns. These findings highlight a nuanced interplay of anticipatory bias and post-award evaluation stringency, reflecting dynamic prestige biases in peer review with implications for editorial fairness, academic evaluation policies, and the efficient dissemination of scientific knowledge.
